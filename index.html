Top Case · Internal Data Analysis · 2026

Building a Conversational Quality Culture: From CSAT Analysis to an AI Metrics Architecture

Company
Conexa Group
Industry
Healthcare Tech
Role
UX Researcher
Duration
1 sprint
Year
2026

The Context

As Conexa AI matured as a product, a critical gap emerged: the team had satisfaction data, but no structured way to understand why the experience was failing — or succeeding. CSAT scores existed, but the signal was noisy and the path from data to decision was unclear.

This case documents how I turned a raw satisfaction dataset into a diagnostic research framework, and from there, into a causal metrics architecture that now guides how Conexa measures conversational quality.

The Research Question

How do we move from reactive satisfaction monitoring to proactive conversational quality management — and what does it take to build the infrastructure to get there?

Phase 1 — Diagnosing the CSAT

I led an analysis of CSAT responses collected over three months (Jan–Mar 2026), combining quantitative data with qualitative analysis of open comments. Analysis was conducted with AI assistance (Claude Opus 4.6).

85%
of comments were noise — unrelated to the AI conversational experience itself
3 pts
gap in rating between users who got their problem solved and those who didn't
r=0.79
correlation between resolutivity and satisfaction — the dominant driver

The first finding was methodological: we couldn't accurately evaluate the AI's experience because we weren't isolating it from the service layer. One of the immediate recommendations was to redesign the CSAT survey to separate AI experience from platform and service evaluation.

Phase 2 — The Non-Obvious Insight

I conducted a deep-dive comparison between two conversations with extreme ratings — one CSAT 1, one CSAT 5 — both with users who commented specifically on the AI's communication style.

The surface-level read: one user found the AI warm and empathetic, the other cold and robotic. Both conversations used the same linguistic repertoire — emojis, name-based greetings, closing phrases.

What the data actually showed: The difference wasn't tone — it was competence and contextual calibration. Perceived artificiality in conversational AI is not primarily a language problem — it is a symptom of performance failure.

This insight reframed the product direction: before investing in tone adjustments, the priority should be resolving the structural failures that make tone irrelevant.

Phase 3 — From Insight to Architecture

I designed a 4-layer causal metrics architecture that maps the chain of causality in a conversational interaction:

Layer 1 — Understanding

Did the AI correctly interpret what the user wanted? · Intent Match Rate · Context Utilization Rate · Clarification Rate

Layer 2 — Decision Quality

Did the AI take the right action for that context? · Misrouting Rate · Override Failure Rate

Layer 3 — Conversation Efficiency

Did the user reach their goal directly? · Task Completion Rate · Turns to Resolution · Drop-off Rate · Loop Detection Rate

Layer 4 — Perceived Outcome

How did the user experience the interaction overall? · CSAT (redesigned) · Resolutivity · Perceived Latency

Status & Next Steps

The metrics architecture was delivered in April 2026 and is currently in instrumentation. Once live, it will enable the team to move from reactive CSAT monitoring to proactive conversational quality management — identifying where the experience breaks before it surfaces in satisfaction scores.

Top Case · Conversational Design · 2026

Conversational Design & Onboarding: Redesigning the First Interaction with Conexa AI

Company
Conexa Group
Role
AI Content Designer
Duration
2 sprints
Methods
Conversation Analysis · Benchmarking · Intent Matrix

What is Conexa AI

Conexa AI is a 24/7 virtual health assistant designed to integrate, organize, and accompany the full patient health journey. It connects people to the care they already have and the care they still need — bringing benefits, professionals, and health history into one place.

The Problem

The original onboarding experience was creating unnecessary friction from the very first interaction. It was overloaded with information, poorly contextualized, and demanded login before the user had even expressed a need — a pattern that went directly against the behavior of real users.

Research — Analyzing 1,000 Real Conversations

Before any design decision, I conducted a behavioral analysis of 1,000 initial conversations (10,000 messages) between users and Conexa AI (Feb–Mar 2026) with AI assistance.

~48%
Administrative intent — scheduling, documents, support
~35%
Clinical intent — describing symptoms, seeking guidance
~15%
Exploratory — no clear intent, generic greetings
2–5%
Emergency situations requiring immediate redirection

The key insight: Over 80% of users already know what they need when they open the conversation. For them, a structured, explanatory onboarding creates friction — not value.

The Design Strategy

Based on the research, I defined the foundational principle of the new experience: Intent-First Interaction — instead of the traditional onboarding-first model.

  • Always respond to intent first — the AI prioritizes identifying and responding to the user's need from the very first message
  • Encourage authentication contextually — it appears only when the requested action actually requires it, framed as a natural step to fulfill the user's need

Architecture & Intent Matrix

The system is built on two layers:

Session Layer — builds context by aggregating five dimensions: identity, entry type (new/returning/lapsed), usage history, eligibility, and health data. Output: a Session Context Object.

Intent Router — receives the Session Context Object + user's first message, detects intent, and determines which conversational flow to activate — including tone, format, and authentication timing.

From this architecture, I mapped 33 distinct scenarios across 7 intent groups — covering every meaningful combination of authentication status, user recurrence, and intent type.

Next Steps

The conversational design and architecture are complete, fully documented, and aligned with the development team. Once live, we'll track acceptance rates, drop-off patterns, and authentication conversion to validate the intent-first hypothesis quantitatively.

Top Case · Card Sorting Study · 2025

What Do Physicians Really Need? A Card Sorting Study Across 12 Specialties

Company
Conexa Group
Role
UX Researcher
Method
Card Sorting (MoSCoW)
Participants
35 physicians · 12 specialties

The Context

The medical record (prontuário) is one of the most strategic and friction-heavy flows in Conexa's physician platform. Complaints were consistent — too many fields, poor organization, and a layout that didn't reflect how different specialists actually work.

The Research Question

Is there a solid evidence base for building specialty-specific medical record templates, with fields ordered according to their practical clinical relevance?

The initial hypothesis: it would be possible to build optimized record models by specialty. The research set out to validate — or refute — that assumption with concrete evidence.

Method

I designed and led a closed, unmoderated card sorting study with 35 physicians across 12 specialties (General Practice, Psychiatry, Family Medicine, Endocrinology, Orthopedics, Pediatrics, and more). Each participant categorized 27 medical record fields using a priority framework inspired by the MoSCoW method. Data analysis was conducted with AI assistance (Gemini 2.5).

Key Findings

  • Only 3 fields achieved universal consensus (>80%) across all specialties: Medical History (HDA), Prescription, and Clinical Conduct
  • 82.9% of physicians considered CIAP 2 unnecessary — a field present in the default layout
  • 66.7% of fields showed high variation between specialties, making a one-size-fits-all model structurally inviable
  • 100% of specialties showed significant internal disagreement — even within the same specialty, physicians diverge on what matters
  • The most extreme case: in General Practice, one physician considered ~85% of fields essential while another considered only ~30% — a 55% gap within the same specialty

The Strategic Insight

Specialty-based personalization alone is insufficient. A modular architecture is the only scalable solution:

3
Fixed universal core fields: HDA, Prescription, Clinical Conduct
~12
Specialty-suggested optional fields (template starting point)
3×
More impact from individual vs. specialty-based personalization

Impact on Product Decisions

  • Served as the evidence base for specialty template construction, defining which fields to include by default per specialty
  • Was a key input in the decision not to rely solely on specialty personalization, pushing toward individual customization as the primary model
  • Shaped the field ordering logic in the new medical record layout, based on clinical relevance by specialty rather than a generic hierarchy
Expert Interviews · Internal Data Analysis · 2024

Redesigning Strategic Mental Health Assessments

Company
Dasa
Role
UX Researcher
Methods
Internal Data Analysis · Expert & User Interviews

The Context

The company's strategic focus was the unification of platforms and mental health services. Within this context, it became essential to reconstruct the mental health stratification process to ensure a complete migration of the service while maintaining a functional journey.

Main Objective

Understand how patients perceive, interact with, and feel when completing, tracking, and accessing their mental health assessments on the health platform — and adjust the experience to minimize negative impacts and design a warmer experience for this stage of the journey.

Methodology

Two research phases, designed to build hypotheses cost-effectively and then validate them:

Phase 1 — Internal Data Analysis: Internal interviews with support teams to understand existing patient reports. Parallel observation of the stratification process with employees experiencing it for the first time.

Phase 2 — Expert Interviews with Mental Health Screening Professionals: Validation and hypothesis building based on what psychology professionals had observed when applying these questionnaires to patients.

Main Findings

The beginning of seeking psychological treatment is a delicate moment. Analysis of internal data alongside interviews made it evident that the initial stage of recognizing the need for therapeutic treatment is a particularly sensitive period for patients.

Mental health assessment questions tend to evoke strong emotional responses. The questions evoke a range from anxiety and fear to satisfaction and happiness.

The patients' greatest need is for warmth and support. Especially after receiving their results, patients needed support and care — and the current journey did not address this adequately.

Key Highlights

  • Hypothesis about high risk of mental health assessments triggering negative emotions validated
  • Identification of key emotional trigger points in the user journey
  • Identification of user pain points and needs throughout this journey

Business Outcomes

  • Increase in consultation conversion (more targeted journey)
  • Increase in recurrence (mental health follow-up)
  • Cross-sell of other company products
  • Greater predictability of treatment duration

Output

Based on the data collected, validated hypotheses, and insights, it was possible to rethink the experience at this stage of the journey — including improvements in verbal and visual language, addition of a CTA and scheduling flow after results, and user guidance and information at critical moments.

Scaling UX Research: Building the Research Structure, Process and Infrastructure Across Product Design

Company
Dasa
Role
UX Researcher
Method
Research Ops

Overview

I built the UX Research practice inside the Product Design team from the ground up. Before this initiative, research was fragmented, inconsistent, and lacked structure, visibility, and strategic influence. I established the operational foundation, upskilled the team, and created a scalable system that now supports evidence-based decision-making across the organization.

What I Built

  • Cross-team research processes between Product Design and Product Management, ensuring research cadence within product cycles and generating strategic impact for both product direction and business outcomes
  • Research Repository including a library of key insights, documentation of ongoing and past projects, a repository of atomic insights for cross-squad visibility, and roadmaps aligned with company OKRs
  • A full research tooling ecosystem, configured and standardized for the team, including tools such as Maze and Dovetail
  • A mentoring and upskilling program enabling every designer to run high-quality, end-to-end UX Research with autonomy
  • A living knowledge base with guides, UX Research principles, and best practices for non-researchers
